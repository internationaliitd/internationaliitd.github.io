Ishan Tewari

Q1) Can you describe a situation in which you felt it necessary to be an ambassador for your India? For example, has there been a situation in which you needed to clarify erroneous stereotypes or assumptions about our home culture? How did you handle that situation?

    The student body at Telecom Bretagne is extremely multicultural with over 60% international students. People sometimes have stereotypes about a place and the people who originate from their, but often realise they are wrong they meet actual people from that country/place. This is true about India and Indians also (the infamous Indian accent) like we have stereotypes of people from certain places. This is more so because of a lack of exposure and the fact that India is still seen as slightly “exotic”. This is why exchange programmes are extremely important as we get to meet people from a variety of cultures and actually get to know about them. Therefore my whole semester, I was implicitly an ambassador for India and not only in particular situations.

Q2) Did you experience a culture shock during the exchange? If yes, how did you deal with it 

    Having lived abroad for a good part of my childhood, living abroad in France for a semester was not too much of a cultural shock for me, although every place has its quirks which makes the experience all the more special.

Q3) What are the major problems that you had to tackle while on exchange? Did they hinder your development or were they a part of your growth as an individual? (Keywords - Language, Accommodation, Transportation, Food, Staff, Profs, People in general etc.)

    My semester abroad at Telecom Bretagne has been pretty smooth so far. I wasn’t very fluent in French before coming to France, so conversing initially with people who didn’t speak english was a slight challenge but never an outright dealbreaker. French people generally are extremely patient and understanding and try to help you out even if you explain in broken french. Apart from that student housing on campus is pretty comfortable and an on campus restaurant and cafe serve meals.

Q4)How has your exchange experience helped you to improve your skillsets, be it academic, extra-curricular, interpersonal etc.? Could you provide specific examples?

The exchange semester has helped me mature as an individual. I have learnt how to work in multicultural group and have become more broad minded. I believe the greatest takeaway this semester has been in terms of interpersonal skills. I participated in activities and studies and interacted with people from all corners of this world, on campus or while travelling on weekends.

Q5)Has your experience in the host country helped you gain an appreciation of how the world is interconnected on all levels (environmental, sociocultural and geopolitical)? If so, can you cite specific examples?

    Studying in France, in one of the best institutions of the European Union has taught the importance of harmony and cooperation at the global level. Because France is a part of the EU, there is increased collaboration between the constituent countries with professors from all its member states teaching/researching here. I think this is becoming increasingly important given the current global economic climate and have learnt to appreciate it more.

Q6)What are the biggest differences you see between the college experience here and in your host country? What did you like the most/the least about your host country?

    I personally feel education at TB is slightly more holistic and practical oriented. While courses at IIT teach us theoretical aspects of courses in extreme detail, courses here are more concerned with their practical implementation and less theoretical, focussing more on real-world scenarios, which I feel can be more relevant at times. That said they can be said to be lacking a sense of completeness which is something I’ve never felt about courses at IIT.
